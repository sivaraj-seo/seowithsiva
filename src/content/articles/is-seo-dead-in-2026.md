Every few years, someone declares SEO dead. In 2012 it was Penguin. In 2016 it was voice search. In 2019 it was featured snippets stealing clicks. None of those killed SEO — they changed it.

The 2025-2026 version of this conversation centres on AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini — and for the first time, there's a real structural shift worth taking seriously. Not because SEO is dead, but because search behaviour is genuinely bifurcating in ways that matter for traffic.

## What's Actually Happening to Search Traffic

The data tells a nuanced story. Google's overall search volume is still growing. Organic click-through rates on traditional results have declined for informational queries — particularly those now answered by AI Overviews — but transactional and navigational queries are largely unaffected. Studies from early 2025 show AI Overview click-through rates vary wildly by query type: informational queries can see 30–60% CTR drops, while commercial queries show minimal change.

Meanwhile, ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Bing AI are handling a growing share of research-phase queries — the "explain this to me" and "compare these options" queries that used to flow to SEO content. These platforms don't send referral traffic in the traditional sense; they send occasional high-intent clicks when users want to verify a source, but mostly they answer in-platform.

So is SEO dead? No. Is the traffic that used to come from certain query types now going to AI platforms instead of your blog? Yes, and it's growing.

## What Traditional SEO Still Wins

Let's be concrete about where classic SEO tactics remain highly effective in 2026:

**Transactional and commercial queries** — "buy running shoes under ₹5,000", "best CRM for startups", "book a hotel in Ooty" — still resolve through traditional SERPs with high click-through rates. AI Overviews appear less frequently on high-commercial-intent queries because Google's business model depends on those clicks.

**Local search** — "dentist near me", "SEO agency in Coimbatore" — still resolves primarily through Google Maps and local packs. GEO/AEO have minimal relevance here; local SEO signals (GBP, citations, reviews) still dominate.

**Branded queries** — your company name, your products, your people — will always route through search. Brand SEO, ORM, and Wikipedia/Wikidata management matter more than ever because AI systems surface brand information from these authoritative sources.

**News and timely content** — AI systems are not equipped to cover breaking events in real time. Topical authority in your niche, combined with fast publishing and structured news schema, still drives significant SEO traffic.

**Deep technical content** — tutorials, documentation, code examples, API references — still attract high-intent searchers who click through to read the full resource. AI can't replace a working code snippet in context.

## Where GEO & AEO Actually Add Value

GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation) and AEO (Answer Engine Optimisation) matter most in specific contexts. You don't need to rebuild your entire content operation around them — but ignoring them entirely is a mistake if any of these apply to your situation:

**You produce informational content at scale.** If your site's traffic engine is how-to guides, comparison articles, FAQs, and explanatory content, this is exactly the query type being captured by AI Overviews. GEO practices — direct-answer formatting, FAQ schema, passage-level clarity — help you get cited rather than replaced.

**You're in a research-heavy or considered-purchase category.** Healthcare, financial services, B2B software, education — industries where buyers do extensive research before converting. Your content needs to show up in the research phase, wherever that now happens.

**Your brand needs to be known, not just found.** AI citation is a form of brand exposure. When Perplexity or Gemini recommends your brand in response to a category query, that's brand-building at scale. GEO and brand entity optimisation create this.

**You're targeting enterprise or professional audiences.** Professionals increasingly use AI assistants as research tools. If your buyers are asking ChatGPT or Copilot about their problems, you want to be the cited answer.

## GEO & AEO: What They Actually Mean in Practice

These terms get thrown around loosely. Here's what they mean operationally:

**AEO (Answer Engine Optimisation)** is the practice of structuring content so it can be directly extracted as an answer by AI systems, voice assistants, and featured snippets. Concretely: FAQ schema, direct-answer opening paragraphs, HowTo markup, Speakable schema, and content structured around specific questions.

**GEO (Generative Engine Optimisation)** is broader — it's the practice of optimising for citation and accurate representation across generative AI platforms including ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, and Bing Copilot. It includes AEO tactics plus entity optimisation, training-data presence, third-party citation building, and off-page brand authority signals.

Neither requires abandoning traditional SEO. They're additive layers on top of a solid SEO foundation.

## A Framework for Deciding What to Prioritise

If your SEO budget and bandwidth are constrained, here's a practical prioritisation model:

1. **Fix technical SEO first.** Crawlability, indexation, Core Web Vitals, and schema basics. These improve both Google rankings and AI citation eligibility simultaneously.

2. **Audit your most-trafficked informational content.** Reformat the top 20 pages for direct-answer structure. Add FAQ schema. This is your highest-leverage GEO/AEO investment.

3. **Invest in local SEO if you have physical locations.** GEO/AEO have almost zero relevance here; GBP optimisation is still the right priority.

4. **Build your brand entity.** Consistent NAP across directories, Wikidata claims, press mentions, author bios with credential verification. This benefits both Google E-E-A-T and LLM citation authority.

5. **Track AI visibility alongside organic traffic.** Use tools like seoClarity or manual prompt testing to monitor whether your brand is being cited in AI answers for your target queries.

## The Honest Answer

SEO is not dead. But "do whatever worked in 2020" is no longer sufficient advice.

The smart move is a portfolio approach: maintain strong traditional SEO practices (they still drive the majority of organic traffic for most sites), add GEO/AEO optimisation to your informational content, and invest in brand entity signals that benefit you across every search and AI platform simultaneously.

The companies treating this as an either/or choice are making a mistake. The ones building for both are quietly capturing traffic from two different pools at the same time.
